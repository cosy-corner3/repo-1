🖌️ Why I Love Gouache

A cozy note from a brush-and-breakup recovery arc.

I’ve always been a creative kid — the kind who lived for art class, painted the standard scenery with snow-peaked triangles and coconut trees, and proudly came home with messy fingers.

One summer at my grandparents’ house, I copied a cartoon from a Tinkle comic, and it looked just like the original. That’s when I knew I had something.

Then school happened. Boards, deadlines, engineering prep… and I kind of lost touch with art. Until one day — thanks to Instagram reels, heartbreak, and a pandemic — I found my way back.

I was going through a rough time during Covid, and on a whim, I bought myself a set of Brustro gouache paints and brushes. Definitely a splurge for a beginner, but hey — good tools matter. I started watching Insta artists paint dreamy skies and tiny cottages, soaking up all the little tips. My first few pieces were… meh. But the more I painted, the more it felt like me again.

🎨 But Why Gouache?

Honestly, I had no idea what gouache even was when I started. It just kept showing up in my feed — and it looked fun.

Turns out, it’s basically a thicker, more opaque version of watercolours. Still water-activated and beginner-friendly, but with more control and a polished, professional feel.

You can go soft with gradients and dreamy sunsets or bold with vibrant blocks and layered illustrations. It’s forgiving… until it isn’t.

Mistakes are hard to undo once it dries. The paint dries quickly on your palette too — so yes, a little spray bottle is now a permanent part of my setup.

But honestly? I love the hassle. The reactivating. The guesswork. The little imperfections. Because that’s kind of what brought me back to art in the first place.

🧘‍♀️ A Painting Ritual

Each painting takes me an hour or two — sometimes more, especially when I’m obsessing over tiny details like plant silhouettes or ripples on water.

I’m still learning how to get colours right, how they dry darker or lighter with gouache, how to capture flow and texture.

But every time I sit down, plug in my earphones with some lo-fi beats, squeeze out paint onto my palette, and hold that brush — I feel like I’ve exited the real world.

It’s not always easy. But it’s worth it.

That said, sometimes I crave something lighter. Quicker. A break from realism and detail.

That’s how I found pixel art.
